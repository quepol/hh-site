Hillary Hartley

Technologist & civic leader

Hillary Hartley has spent nearly three decades at the intersection of design, technology, and democracy — making government work better for the people it serves. She got her start as a web designer for Arkansas.gov in 1997, and went on to create MyPollingPlace.com, the first nationwide polling-place lookup tool, during the 2004 U.S. election cycle. Before entering federal service, she served as Director of Integrated Marketing at NIC, helping thirty state-government partners adopt 21st-century tools for citizen engagement.

In 2013, Hartley joined the White House as a Presidential Innovation Fellow at the General Services Administration, working on MyUSA — a project to reimagine how Americans interact with their government online. She went on to co-found 18F, GSA's pioneering in-house digital services team, growing it from ten people to 250 designers, developers, and product managers delivering critical services for U.S. departments and agencies. That work earned her a spot on Fast Company's 100 Most Creative People of 2015.

In 2017, Hartley made history as Ontario's first-ever Chief Digital and Data Officer — and Deputy Minister for Digital Government — a role she held until 2023. Leading the Ontario Digital Service, she guided one of the largest public-sector digital transformations in North American history, building a team from scratch, operationalizing the landmark Simpler, Faster, Better Services Act (2019), and redesigning services used by millions of Ontarians. Her appointment was covered by BetaKit and Government Technology.

Most recently, Hartley served as CEO of U.S. Digital Response, a nonprofit, nonpartisan organization with a corps of more than 10,000 volunteer technologists who are paired with state and local governments — and the nonprofits that support them — to rapidly deliver technology products and build lasting public-sector capacity. She is now a Catalyst at OpenPublic, and continues to write and speak about digital government, open source, and what endures when the political winds shift. Her work has been documented by the Computer History Museum and covered in FedScoop, Lawfare, and elsewhere.
